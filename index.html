FAQ

General: What does this project do?

It visualizes and compares how countries transition from ICE-dominated new registrations to BEV-dominated new registrations.

General: What exactly is being modeled?

The BEV share of new vehicle registrations over time.

General: Is this a forecast?

No. It is a best-fit description of the transition as of today, conditional on currently observed data.

General: What does the curve mean in plain English?

It shows what the transition looks like if the structure visible in the data continues.

General: Why do curves change over time?

Because the model is refit when new data arrives, so parameters and derived dates update.

General: Why are some countries ahead?

Because adoption drivers differ by taxes, incentives, charging, vehicle supply, income, fleet structure and policy.

General: Why are some countries slower?

Because the observed data shows a slower transition shape or later acceleration.

General: Why does this matter?

It makes different national transitions comparable with one consistent framework.

General: What is BEV share?

The percentage of newly registered vehicles that are battery-electric vehicles.

General: What is being compared across countries?

Transition shape, timing, duration, thresholds and speed.

General: Why not just show raw monthly data?

Because raw monthly data is noisy and hard to compare across markets.

General: Why is the project public?

To make transition dynamics transparent and accessible.

Data: Where does the data come from?

Data comes from multiple sources, with ACEA being the largest. Where possible, original national statistical agencies or ministries are used.

Data: How often is the data updated?

The dataset is updated whenever new official registration data becomes available and the models are re-run.

Data: Are all countries based on monthly data?

No. Some countries provide monthly data, others only quarterly or yearly data depending on the source.

Data: What is TTM?

TTM stands for trailing twelve months. It represents a rolling 12-month view of market shares.

Data: Why use TTM instead of monthly values?

TTM smooths seasonal effects and short-term volatility, making underlying trends easier to interpret.

Data: Why do some countries show BEV / PHEV / HEV separately while others only show HYBRID?

Statistical agencies publish different category structures. Some split hybrids into plug-in hybrids (PHEV) and conventional hybrids (HEV), while others only provide a combined hybrid category. Definitions also vary widely (e.g. treatment of mild hybrids or fuel splits).
Harmonizing categories across countries is inherently imperfect. In practice, BEV / PHEV / ICE tends to provide the most consistent cross-country comparison.

Data: Why are country categories inconsistent?

Different statistical agencies use different definitions and reporting standards for drivetrain categories, which leads to inconsistencies across countries.

Data: Why are some data sources better than others?

Some sources provide cleaner splits between BEV, PHEV and ICE and offer more frequent updates, making them more suitable for modeling.

Data: What happens if a country only reports BEV, Hybrid and ICE?

The model adapts to the available categories, but interpretation must take into account that hybrids are not split into PHEV and HEV.

Data: Why can shares differ slightly from official statistics?

Shares are sometimes recalculated from category totals to ensure internal consistency. In addition, agencies may revise past months, use different vehicle scopes (e.g. passenger cars vs. total vehicles), and apply non-harmonized fuel categories.
By default, this site focuses on passenger vehicles.

Data: What does “Updated to” mean?

It indicates the latest time period for which data is included in the model for a given country.

Data: What is the base data used for modeling?

Absolute registration numbers are used as the base and then converted into shares for modeling.

Data: Why are absolute numbers still important?

They are used as weights in the model to reduce distortions from unusually small or volatile months.

Model: What function is used?

A Weibull-style cumulative transition function is used to model BEV adoption over time.

Model: What is the mathematical form of the model?

The core function is f(x) = 1 - exp(v1 · x^v2), combined with a time shift parameter to align the curve with calendar time.

Model: Why use a Weibull-type model?

It allows flexible, asymmetric S-curves and makes it obvious when the data does not support a meaningful transition.

Model: Why not use a normal distribution?

A normal distribution is symmetric, while real-world adoption processes are typically asymmetric.

Model: Why not use a linear model?

Technology transitions are nonlinear and bounded, making linear models unsuitable.

Model: Why assume an S-curve?

Most technology transitions start slowly, accelerate, and then saturate, which is well captured by S-shaped curves.

Model: Is the model monotonic?

Yes. The model enforces a monotonic transition from low to high BEV share.

Model: What is the lower bound of the model?

Conceptually 0% BEV share at the start of the transition.

Model: What is the upper bound of the model?

Conceptually 100% BEV share in the long run.

Model: Why enforce 0% and 100% bounds?

It stabilizes the model and ensures comparability across countries.

Model: How many parameters does the model have?

Two shape parameters (v1 and v2) plus a time shift parameter.

Model: Why only two shape parameters?

Fewer parameters would be too rigid, while more would increase instability and overfitting.

General: Why does electrification sometimes appear slow?

Fleet turnover is slow. Even if BEV sales grow rapidly, replacing the existing vehicle fleet takes many years. Cars on the road do not disappear just because new registrations are electric. With typical lifespans of around 10–15 years, the transition of the fleet naturally lags behind the transition in new sales.

General: Why do some countries electrify faster than others?

Adoption speed depends on many factors: vehicle supply, charging infrastructure, electricity and fuel prices, income levels, taxation, media perception and more. There is no single dominant driver. However, strong purchase incentives from governments consistently accelerate BEV adoption.

General: Why does adoption accelerate after a certain point?

Technology adoption typically follows an S-curve: slow at first, then accelerating as barriers fall, and eventually slowing again as saturation is approached. This acceleration is gradual, not tied to a specific moment. The model visualizes this continuous process rather than a discrete tipping point.

Model: How are fleet projections calculated?

Fleet projections combine the last observed fleet composition with modeled inflow of new vehicles and category-specific attrition rates.

Model: How are fleet projections calculated in detail?

The model projects the fleet forward year by year using three components: (1) the existing fleet from the previous year, (2) category-specific retirement ("hazard") rates, and (3) the annual inflow of newly registered vehicles.

Example starting fleet (2025):
BEV: 945,182
PHEV: 208,467
HEV: 162,422
DIESEL: 942,780
PETROL: 676,614
OTHERS: 270

Example hazard (retirement) rates:
BEV: 0.010
PHEV: 0.020
HEV: 0.030
HYBRID: 0.030
DIESEL: 0.065
PETROL: 0.055
OTHERS: 0.050

Assume:
Annual new vehicle inflow: 150,000
Inflow multiplier: 1
Net imports: 0

Step 1 – Apply hazard rates (vehicles leaving the fleet).
Each category loses a fraction of its vehicles according to its hazard rate.
Example:
2026 BEV survivors = 945,182 × (1 − 0.010)

Step 2 – Add new vehicle inflow.
The annual inflow is distributed using the modeled BEV share for the middle of the projection year (year + 0.5).

Example:
2026 BEV fleet = 945,182 × (1 − 0.010) + 150,000 × BEV_share(2026.5)

Step 3 – Assign the remaining inflow.
Non-BEV inflow is assigned as follows:
• If PHEV exists → to PHEV
• Else if HYBRID exists → to HYBRID
• Else → to OTHERS

The process repeats each year, using the updated fleet as the new baseline.

Model: How are new vehicle registrations assigned to fleet categories in the projections?

Annual inflow is split using the modeled BEV and ICE shares at the midpoint of the projection year (year + 0.5). BEV inflow goes to BEV. The remaining non-BEV inflow is assigned to PHEV if available, otherwise to HYBRID, and only falls back to OTHERS if neither category exists.

Model: Why do some countries show inflow into PHEV or HYBRID instead of OTHERS?

The model follows the category structure of the underlying dataset. If PHEV exists, non-BEV inflow is assigned there. If not, but HYBRID exists, it is assigned to HYBRID. This ensures consistency between projected and observed fleet categories.

Region / World Curve Builder

This builder aggregates national BEV transition models into regional or global curves. Multiple countries are stacked into a single trajectory.
Aggregation is weighted by market size using weights.csv, ensuring that large markets dominate the result while small markets contribute proportionally.
The x-axis uses index time internally (see t0 parameter). Minor rounding artefacts are expected due to time conversion and general limitation while rounding, since these calculations run client side on your device.
The builder defaults to passenger cars (= Whole) (4-Wheelers = legacy for India) in case there are no parameters for a variant like HDV or Vans.
If the variant exists (for example in Thresholds or Durations Tab), then the Builder will create the variant curve.
